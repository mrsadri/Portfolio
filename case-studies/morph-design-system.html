Origin

Morph was not commissioned. It started as a practical need: in 2019, our team was on Adobe XD with painful handoff and no shared design language. Migrating to Figma surfaced that every product had drifted into its own visual conventions.

The core idea: as we move to Figma, build a design system that does both jobs at once. Standardise the design language and establish a reusable component library. SetareYek (the consumer fintech app) would be the primary consumer.

  • v1 (Jan 2020): internal release. Used to redesign the SetareYek mobile app, corporate dashboards, and marketing sites.
  • Public release (~1 year later): the company saw what we'd built and gave us permission to open-source it as a gift to the broader Persian-speaking design community, with workshop budget to promote it.

Adaptable, swift, integrated. Morph emphasises design principles such as accessibility, with adaptability across mobile applications, admin panels, and websites in RTL and LTR, and across light and dark palettes.

What Made Morph Different

By 2020, there were dozens of public Figma design systems. Most were Material ports or English-first kits. Morph filled a real gap.

Four Differentiators
  • RTL-first, not RTL-as-afterthought. Persian as the primary language, with LTR built as a parallel mode rather than a transformation. Bilingual designers (Persian + English, Persian + Arabic) got a system that worked in both directions without breaking.
  • HSL-based theming, before Figma had variables. We defined colours by role (primary, surface, accent) and shifted the whole palette by parameter. Dark mode wasn't a manual repaint; it was a parameter change. When Figma later introduced variables, our system mapped onto them cleanly.
  • Accessibility tested on every component. I personally ran a11y plugins (contrast checking) and blind-testing tools (screen reader compatibility, tab order). Accessibility was a guardrail, not a feature.
  • The largest Persian/RTL design system available. 4,000+ icons, 864 button variants, 1,488 text field variants, 240 modal variants, plus tags, snack bars, tables, navigation bars, tab bars, search boxes. Community feedback consistently called out consistency as Morph's defining quality.

My Role & the Team

I was the most experienced Figma practitioner on the team and managed the project's architecture. Hossein Mahmoodvand (UX team manager) co-led with me. Mohammad Hossein Ahmadi was our existing visual designer.

The expansion required hiring. Hossein and I interviewed 40+ candidates through a structured process: CV review (both of us) → take-home assignment (I designed and judged the task) → final interview (I led; Hossein joined for some) → joint offer decision.

We hired 3 new junior designers. Two became core to Morph's expansion: Mohammad Mahdi Mohebbi (intern, his first job in design) and Amirhossein Nazari.

Delegation philosophy
  • For the first few weeks I taught Figma fundamentals, design system architecture, and review workflows, using Morph components as the curriculum.
  • They also learned usability testing methods I was running in parallel product projects, so they wouldn't graduate as "design system specialists" with no product experience.
  • After onboarding, I delegated whole components to them: they owned design decisions; I reviewed. The goal wasn't to ship faster (I could have built it alone faster). The goal was to develop them.
  • Both interns are still working in design today. That's the credit I'm most proud of from this project.
Architectural Decisions That Mattered
Decision 01

HSL over RGB

The problem: Multi-theme support (dark mode, brand variations) without manual repainting and without Figma variables (which didn't exist yet).

Why HSL won: A colour like hsl(220, 60%, 50%) is composable: change the lightness and you have the same colour darker; change the hue and you have a sister colour at the same saturation/lightness. RGB has none of this. Dark mode became "shift lightness across the palette" instead of "manually repaint 200 components."

What I had to defend: Other team members initially preferred RGB because it's what they were used to. I demonstrated the maintenance cost difference on a small sample before the team agreed.

Decision 02

Cut calendar and clock from v1

The pressure: Persian (Jalali) calendar is a hard problem, with different month structure, week start, and cultural conventions. A proper calendar would have been a project of its own.

The trade-off: Shipping with 95% coverage on schedule beat 100% coverage six months late. I documented these as "known gaps for future versions."

What I learned: The right call. Public reception focused on the components we did ship; nobody complained about missing calendar. Scope discipline mattered more than feature completeness.

Decision 03

CC BY 4.0 open license

The defence: The company gave us permission to open-source, though they could have asked for a more restrictive license. I argued for CC BY 4.0 because (a) it would maximise adoption (and therefore community goodwill), (b) attribution requirement still gave Setare Aval visibility, and (c) restrictive licenses create hesitation in adoption because designers wouldn't trust they could use it on commercial client work.

Why it mattered: This decision is the reason 8.7K designers have adopted Morph and a bank like Blu Bank could quietly use it. A more restrictive license would have suppressed adoption.

Unofficial commercial adoption
  • Blu Bank (regional neobank) used Morph as the foundation for their product UI; they let us know through an informal call.
  • Multiple other regional companies have used Morph unofficially, within license terms but without explicit notification.
  • A volunteer developer built a documentation website for Morph (later shut down).
Internal impact at Setare Aval
  • Adobe XD → Figma migration completed across all design teams.
  • SetareYek mobile app, Setare Aval marketing sites, and internal dashboards rebuilt on Morph.
  • Engineering handoff time reduced (qualitative; no metric captured at the time).
What I'd Do Differently
  • Capture the engineering handoff metric. I have qualitative evidence Morph improved handoff speed and reduced UI bugs at Setare Aval; the team said so, repeatedly. I never captured the metric. A senior design system lead establishes a baseline and measures it.
  • Build governance, not just components. Morph shipped as a component library, not with a contribution model, versioning protocol, or deprecation policy. By the time the volunteer documentation site launched, we needed those, and we didn't have them. A design system isn't a one-time artefact; it's an ongoing relationship.
  • Document the HSL decision publicly. The HSL theming approach was Morph's most distinctive technical choice. We never wrote about it. If we had, Morph would have been part of the conversation when Figma rolled out variables.
