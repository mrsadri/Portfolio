From 6% to 47%: Turning a Failing Feature Into Core Value

How I redesigned the SetareYek bill payment flow to grow market share 6%→47% in 4.5 months

I came in expecting one blocker. I found six. The fix wasn't a faster flow; it was a different mental model. The redesign required one production rollback, three iterations, and two self-proposed growth features the PM hadn't asked for.

The Problem

Bill payment was underused. Users had the feature but avoided it, choosing to pay through banks, ATMs, or external apps instead.

Baseline market share: 6% of bill payment volume, measured internally against other SetareYek payment categories.

The strategic stakes: if SetareYek didn't make in-app bill payment compelling soon, banks and dedicated fintech apps would lock in the habit. The PM wanted me to find out why users weren't using it, and then fix it.

  • High drop-off at specific steps in the payment flow
  • Users spending unusually long on single screens
  • Many abandoned attempts before payment confirmation
  • Users who'd paid once rarely returning to pay again

"People pay bills every month; it's not optional. So why are they avoiding our app?"

The research question I started with

The opportunity: Bill payment is a high-frequency, high-stress task every household faces monthly. If we could own it inside the app, we'd become indispensable.

The problem: The feature existed. Almost nobody used it.

Success Metrics

North star and non-negotiable guardrails defined before a single wireframe.

North star: % of total SetareYek internal payment volume going through in-app bill payment, vs. other payment categories.

Non-negotiable guardrails
  • Must not break the existing flow for power users
  • Must work on the spectrum of devices used across Iran (low-end Android included)
  • Must not introduce new friction in pursuit of new features
  • Receipt sharing must satisfy landlords (verifiable by non-app users)
Constraints & Tradeoffs

Four constraints shaped every design decision in this project.

1
Government-regulated payment codes

I couldn't change the 29 digits users had to enter, since that was set by national infrastructure. I had to design around the constraint, not eliminate it. Solution: enter once, store, refresh automatically.

2
Trust gap with landlords

Landlords are non-users of the app, but they're gatekeepers of whether tenants can use the feature. Any digital receipt had to be verifiable by someone who'd never installed SetareYek.

3
Loss of administrative paper

Killing the paper bill created real-world problems for users (property sales, ADSL registration). The design had to make the digital version more useful than paper, not just equivalent.

4
3 iterations only

Each iteration cost roughly 2–3 weeks of dev queue time. I couldn't afford more than three swings before the strategic window closed. This made the Iteration 1 failure especially costly.

Discovery: 6 Frustrations, Not 1

I started expecting to find one main blocker. What I found was structural.

I ran in-depth interviews with building managers, heads of households, and everyday users, combined with session recordings, drop-off analysis, and contextual inquiry. That meant observing bill payment in real contexts, including a building manager's physical notebook.

Six distinct frustrations were each contributing to abandonment:

# Frustration What I learned
1 Flow too difficult Users had to enter a 13-digit bill code and a 16-digit payment code every time, then request the bill. Pure friction, from scratch each monthly cycle.
2 No proof for landlords Users preferred paper receipts because landlords asked for proof. Online payment broke this trust loop between tenant and landlord.
3 Physical bills got lost Municipalities mailed bills, users misplaced them, and then couldn't pay because they had no codes. No fallback.
4 Forgetting to pay on time No reminder system. Late fees were a constant complaint, and a reason to distrust the app after the fact.
5 Paper had administrative value Users needed paper bills as proof for selling property or registering for ADSL internet. Going digital felt like losing legal leverage.
6 No payment history Users wanted to see what they'd paid and when, for both memory and documentation. The app had no record of past payments.

The Strategic Reframe

The lift came from this reframe, not from the visual design.

Bill payment couldn't be designed as a faster button. It had to be designed as an inventory system: a place where users' bills lived, updated themselves, and could be paid, shared, and referenced over time.

This single insight changed every downstream decision.

The physical world already had this system. Building managers kept notebooks of bill IDs. Households set calendar reminders. People made photocopies of receipts. The digital design needed to replace each of these behaviours, not just the payment step.

  • Keep notebooks of bill IDs → Digital bill inventory (enter once, stored permanently)
  • Set calendar reminders → Smart due-date notifications
  • Make photocopies of receipts → Shareable official digital receipts with landlord verification
Iteration 1: The Latency Assumption I Missed

I designed iteration 1 in 5 days after 14 days of research. Live for ~24 hours before rollback.

The core idea: a list of bill cards, each showing the current bill price prominently with a "Pay Now" button. Click → pay. Minimal friction.

It went into the dev queue, launched 14 days later. Live for ~24 hours before rollback.

24h
Time to production failure
~24h before rollback

What went wrong

The latency assumption
  • Building a list of bill cards with live price data required fetching the current price for every bill on every list render
  • At scale, this took noticeable time, so the list either loaded slowly or partially
  • Users who'd previously used the old flow opened the new screen, saw nothing, and left
How I detected it
  • The DevOps team identified the bill payment volume drop ~24 hours after launch and flagged it to PM Ali
  • Not fast by my standards now, but fast enough to roll back before the damage compounded
What I should have caught earlier
  • I designed for an idealised state where data was already available. I didn't model the network and rendering cost of fetching live bill prices for every card on first paint.
  • A senior designer should treat latency as a UX dimension, not a technical handoff question.

What I chose NOT to do here: patch the failure and re-launch quickly. The broken state was giving me exactly the insight I needed about what the system had to handle.

Iteration 2: Three Days to Fix

Three architectural changes. Design completed in 3 days; full iteration shipped after standard dev cycle.

"Update Price" instead of "Pay Now" on cards

The card no longer pretended to have live data. Each card showed the saved bill identifier; the price loaded only when the user explicitly tapped "Update Price." This pushed the cost from list-render time to user-initiated action: predictable, controllable, and feedback-clear.

Loading states everywhere

Both the list view and individual cards now had explicit loading states. No more empty screens that looked broken. Users always knew the app was working.

Saved bill inventory as the core mental model

Users entered the 29 digits once, named the bill (e.g. "Apartment electricity"), and from then on the bill lived in their inventory. Update price → pay → keep history. The inventory system concept fully implemented.

Frustrations resolved at this stage: #1 (flow too difficult, via persistent identifiers) and #3 (physical bills lost, via the inventory itself) were solved at the architectural level here, not at the feature level.

Iteration 3: Full Solution + Two Growth Features

With the inventory architecture stable, I addressed the remaining 4 frustrations, and proposed two growth features that weren't in the PM's brief.

Solving the remaining 4 frustrations

Receipt sharing for landlords

Users could share digital receipts directly. I wrote in-receipt verification instructions so landlords, who are non-app users, could verify authenticity through Setare Aval's public verification page. Solved frustrations #2 and #5 simultaneously: digital receipts now had administrative weight.

Time-ordered bill list

Bills sorted by time-to-pay, with the most urgent on top. Solved frustration #4 (forgetting to pay on time).

Full payment history

Every paid bill stayed in a searchable history view. Solved frustration #6 (no record of what was paid and when).

Persistent bill identifiers

Once entered, the 13- and 16-digit codes never had to be re-entered, since they were tied permanently to that named bill in inventory. Solved frustration #1 at the structural level.

Frustration mapping recap
  • Iteration 2 (architecture) → solved #1 (flow too difficult) and #3 (physical bills lost)
  • Iteration 3 (features) → solved #2 (no proof for landlords), #4 (forgetting to pay on time), #5 (paper had administrative value), #6 (no payment history)
  • All 6 frustrations addressed across the redesign.

Two growth features I proposed (not in PM brief)

During discovery I noticed two patterns the PM hadn't asked about. I held them, validated them through the same research method as the core feature, and proposed them for iteration 3, backed by data, not opportunism.

Feature A
Family package visibility

Research showed that many bill-payers purchased internet packages and top-ups on behalf of family members. The pain: they didn't know when a family member's package would expire, so renewals were late or guesswork. I designed a view showing how much of each purchased package remained, visible to the buyer, not just the recipient. This turned a one-off transaction into a recurring relationship.

Feature B: Auto-referral via gift payment
Pay on behalf of → auto-invite recipient

Extended Feature A: users could pay phone bills on behalf of parents, friends, or family. After a successful gift payment, the app auto-invited the recipient to download SetareYek with a referral code tied to the gift transaction.

Usability Testing

After iteration 3 design but before development, I ran 2 weeks of usability testing solo, the safeguard iteration 1 skipped.

Format: Mixed. Moderated sessions for the core flows (inventory creation, bill update + payment, receipt sharing) and unmoderated tasks for the discovery flows (history search, family package view).

Recruitment: SetareYek's existing user base and personal network, covering both personas: Head of Household and Building Manager.

Four core flows tested: inventory creation, bill update + payment, receipt sharing with landlord verification, payment history search.

What I found and fixed before dev:

  • Tap target issues on the bill cards, especially for the older Building Manager persona
  • The "share" affordance was unclear, so users didn't realise digital receipts could be sent outside the app
  • Verification instruction copy needed simplification for non-technical landlords
This testing window is the reason iteration 3 didn't break in production. Iteration 1 skipped it.

Quantitative results

  • Bill payment market share grew from 6% to 47% of in-app payment share (vs. other SetareYek payment categories) across 3 iterations
  • ~20,000 new active users acquired in first 2 months of the auto-referral feature
  • ~1 in 3 referrals converted from gift payment invitations (~60,000 invitations sent in that window)
  • Total time to peak: ~4.5 months (2.5 months design + development, 2 months adoption ramp)

Qualitative results

  • Users who'd previously churned returned, because the inventory model removed the re-entry friction that drove abandonment
  • Landlords accepted digital receipts, because the verification instructions solved the trust loop outside the app
  • Bill payment became a primary value proposition rather than a neglected feature
What I'd Do Differently
Model latency as a design dimension from the start

I designed for the ideal data state, not the real one. I now treat "what does this look like at the slowest acceptable network speed?" as a required design exercise, not a technical handoff question. Iteration 1 would not have broken in production.

Run a smaller pilot before full rollout

Iteration 1 went to 100% of users at launch. If I'd run it on a 5% cohort first, the price-fetch cost would have shown up the same way but with much less business damage and a faster rollback.

Build the measurement dashboard before iteration 1 ships, not after

We caught the 24-hour drop because DevOps was watching infrastructure metrics, not because I'd instrumented the design for measurement. A senior designer owns the measurement framework as part of the design, not as a post-launch ask.

Key Learnings
1
Reframe before you redesign

I came in expecting "make payment faster." The real solution was "build an inventory system." That reframe, not the visual design, is what drove the 6%→47% lift.

2
Design doesn't exist in isolation

Even perfect UX fails without technical performance. Latency is a design dimension. The 24-hour rollback was a direct consequence of not treating it as one.

3
Emotional needs are functional needs

The receipt wasn't decoration; it was the entire value proposition. A landlord who can't verify a digital payment doesn't accept it. Design the full trust chain, not just the flow.

4
Failure can be your most valuable data

Owning the iteration 1 rollback without defending it, redesigning in 3 days, and using the failure to set testing standards: that was the senior move. The fast rollback mattered more than the fast launch.

5
The brief is the floor, not the ceiling

Family package visibility and auto-referral were my proposals, not the PM's. Both shipped. Auto-referral acquired ~20K active users. Discovery work pays for itself when you use it to see beyond the brief.

A note on PM

Special thanks to Ali YaseriPour, who gave me space to push beyond the original scope. Family Package Visibility and Auto-Referral were not in his original brief, and he backed both proposals against business risk. The 20K acquisitions wouldn't exist without that trust.
