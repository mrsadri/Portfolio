## The Platform

## The Research Challenge

### Why Traditional Methods Wouldn't Work

When users uninstall an app, they're gone. You can't interview them. You can't run usability tests. And even if you could, people rarely tell you the real emotional reasons behind their decisions.

I needed a different approach.

### The Solution: Digital Archaeology

## The Real Problems

Through analyzing these user voices, three critical issues emerged:

### 1. **Trust Deficit**
Users perceived the prize system as rigged or unfair. Without transparency, no amount of marketing could build loyalty.

### 2. **Anti-Viral Behavior**
Users actively **avoided** inviting friends because they believed more participants = lower odds of winning. Our growth mechanism was working against itself.

### 3. **Feature Blindness**
Users saw Setare Aval as "just a lottery app." They had no idea about the utility featuresâ€”bill payments, top-ups, internet packagesâ€”that could make the app indispensable.

**The core insight:** We were solving the wrong problem. The issue wasn't getting users to engage with lotteries. It was getting them to see value **beyond** the lotteries.

## The Solutions

### Solution 1: Turn Users into Gift-Givers

**The Insight:** When you buy a top-up or pay someone else's bill, you're doing them a favor. What if that favor also introduced them to Setare Aval?

**The Mechanism:**

When a user purchases something for another phone number (top-up, internet package, bill payment), the recipient receives an SMS.

**Three Message Variants Tested:**

**Message A** (Control)
> "User 4675----0912 has purchased {Internet package} for you via Setare Yek app. [Setare Yek promotional text]"

**Message B** (Add Download Link)
> "User 4675----0912 has purchased {Internet package} for you via Setare Yek app. [Promotional text] Download: [Link]"

**Message C** (Add Incentive)
> "User 4675----0912 has purchased {Internet package} for you via Setare Yek app. Register and enter invite code {Code} to earn 500 points. Download: [Link]"

**The Psychology:**
- Creates obligation through reciprocity
- Leverages social proof (someone I know uses this)
- Provides immediate value (500 points)
- Makes existing users feel generous, not competitive

---

### Solution 2: Gamified Task System with Real Rewards

**The Problem with Previous Approach:**
- Users only engaged when big prizes were announced
- No ongoing reason to use utility features
- Prize system felt like gambling, not earning

**The New System:**

A weekly task-based reward system that encouraged diverse app usage:

**Task Examples:**
- Pay a utility bill â†’ Earn points
- Buy top-up for someone else â†’ Earn bonus points
- Make your first payment this week â†’ Unlock multiplier

**Key Innovation:** Points weren't just for lottery entriesâ€”they could be withdrawn as real value after completing enough tasks.

## The Design Evolution

### First Version Problems (Discovered in Usability Testing)

âŒ **Checkboxes felt clickable** - Users tried to "complete" tasks by checking them  
âŒ **Progress display unclear** - "Completed: 1 of 2" didn't motivate  
âŒ **Wrong emphasis** - Focused on withdrawing 1,500 points instead of earning more  
âŒ **No task guidance** - Users didn't know how to complete tasks  
âŒ **Unclear connection** - Link between completing tasks and activating withdrawal was confusing

### Critical Feedback from Marketing Team

> "Why limit users weekly? Why not make it incremental?"

**My Response:** Weekly resets create urgency and habit formation. If tasks accumulated indefinitely, users would procrastinate. Weekly cycles mirror natural planning rhythms (weekends, paydays) and create consistent engagement patterns.

---

### Refined Interface Design

**Key Improvements:**

**Visual Task Status**
- Replaced checkboxes with progress indicators
- Clear visual hierarchy: Incomplete â†’ In Progress â†’ Complete
- Direct links to relevant sections (e.g., "Pay Bill" button goes to bill payment)

**Smart Progress Display**
- Changed from "1 of 2" to visual progress bars
- Added point values clearly next to each task
- Showed total potential earnings prominently

**Motivation Architecture**
- Emphasized earning/doubling points over withdrawal
- Made "bonus" opportunities visually distinct
- Added contextual education about point value

**Guided Actions**
- Each incomplete task had a direct action button
- Contextual tooltips explaining benefits
- Preview of what unlocks after task completion

### What Changed

**Before:** Users came for prizes, left when they didn't win  
**After:** Users stayed for utility, prizes became a bonus

**Before:** Users avoided inviting friends (competition)  
**After:** Users invited friends to share value (reciprocity)

**Before:** App was "just for lotteries"  
**After:** App became part of weekly payment routine

## What I Learned

### 1. **Listen to What Users Do, Not Just What They Say**
The 380 comments revealed emotional truths users would never admit in an interview: feeling cheated, distrustful, exploited.

### 2. **Incentives Shape Behavior**
Our lottery system inadvertently made users selfish (don't invite friends) and transactional (only engage when prizes are big). Changing incentives changed everything.

### 3. **Desk Research is Underrated**
When traditional research methods fail (uninstalled users, emotional decisions), user-generated content provides unfiltered insights.

### 4. **Gamification â‰  Games**
Early task system felt like checking boxes. Successful version felt like earning rewards through real value creation.

### 5. **Product Changes Need Protection Windows**
Pausing marketing for 8 weeks was controversial but essential. It proved our solutions worked independently of external spend.

## Reflection
